ADSL AND FTTH: Principles of Operation

ADSL

ADSL (Asymmetric Digital Subscriber Line) is a broadband technology that uses existing copper telephone lines to transmit data. Key principles:

  • Utilizes different frequencies for upstream and downstream data
  • Asymmetric speeds: faster download than upload
  • Uses a splitter to separate voice and data signals
  • Maximum theoretical speed: 24 Mbps download, 3.3 Mbps upload
  • Performance degrades with distance from the telephone exchange

FTTH

FTTH (Fiber to the Home) is a fiber optic communication delivery method. Key principles:

  • Uses fiber optic cables to transmit data as light pulses
  • Offers symmetrical high-speed connections
  • Capable of speeds up to 10 Gbps or more
  • Minimal signal degradation over long distances
  • Requires new infrastructure installation

While ADSL technology leverages existing copper infrastructure to provide broadband access, FTTH represents a significant leap forward in terms of speed, reliability, and future-proofing. FTTH's use of fiber optic technology allows for much higher data transmission rates and lower latency, making it the preferred choice for modern, high-bandwidth applications. However, the widespread adoption of FTTH is limited by the need for new infrastructure installation, which can be costly and time-consuming compared to ADSL's use of existing telephone lines.
